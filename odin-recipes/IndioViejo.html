Indio Viejo

Description:

The "Old Indian" Stew

Ingredients:

For the Meat and Broth:

  • 1 lb Beef (brisket, flank steak, or chuck roast—any cut that shreds easily).
  • 4 cups Water.
  • ½ cup Bean broth (the liquid from the pot).
  • 4 cloves Garlic, smashed.
  • 1 Small onion, halved.
  • 1 tsp Salt.

For the Stew:

  • 1 lb Fresh corn masa (or 2 cups of Maseca corn flour mixed with water).
  • 1 Large white onion, finely chopped.
  • 1 Large green bell pepper (chiltoma), finely chopped.
  • 3 Medium tomatoes, chopped.
  • 1 bunch Fresh spearmint (hierbabuena) — Crucial for the flavor.
  • ½ cup Bitter orange juice (naranja agria) or a mix of lime and orange.
  • 2 tsp Achiote paste (for that signature orange-red color).
  • 3 tbsp Vegetable oil or lard.
  • Salt to taste.

Instructions

  1. Prepare the Beef: Place the beef, garlic, halved onion, and salt in a pot with the water. Bring to a boil, then simmer until the meat is very tender (about 1.5 to 2 hours, or 45 minutes in a pressure cooker).

    • Important Save the broth! Remove the meat and shred it finely with two forks.

  2. Prepare the Masa Base: In a large bowl, dissolve the corn masa (or Maseca) in 2-3 cups of the reserved beef broth. Mix thoroughly until there are no lumps. It should have the consistency of a thick batter. Stir in the achiote paste until the liquid is a uniform orange color.

  3. The Sofrito: In a large, deep pot, heat the oil. Sauté the chopped onion, bell pepper, and tomatoes until they are soft and fragrant. Add the shredded beef and stir for a few minutes so the meat absorbs the flavors of the vegetables.

  4. The Slow Simmer: Pour the masa mixture into the pot with the meat and vegetables. Lower the heat to medium-low.

    • The Golden Rule: You must stir constantly with a wooden spoon. Because of the corn masa, it can stick or burn at the bottom very easily.

  5. The Finishing Touches As the stew thickens (it should look like a thick porridge or heavy puree), add the bitter orange juice and the fresh spearmint leaves. The spearmint is the soul of this dish—don't be afraid to use a lot. Season with salt to taste.

Tips for the Best Indio Viejo:

  • Texture: If it becomes too thick (like a block), add a little more broth. It should be creamy but hold its shape on a plate.

  • The Acid: The "zing" comes from the bitter orange. If you can’t find it, use 2 parts lime juice to 1 part orange juice.

  • Serving: Traditionally served with a slice of lime, white rice, and a few pieces of boiled green plantain or maduros.
